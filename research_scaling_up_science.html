Research Brief

The Science Behind Scaling Up

A critical research synthesis of the academic foundations underlying
the Rockefeller Habits / Gazelles framework by Verne Harnish
Purpose: Evaluate scientific rigor before application — not implementation advice
Date: June 2026
Sources: 40+ academic papers, meta-analyses, and replications
Executive Summary

Verne Harnish’s Scaling Up framework draws—sometimes explicitly, often implicitly—from multiple streams of organizational science. Some underlying research is among the strongest in all of psychology (Locke & Latham’s goal-setting theory, d = 0.52–0.82). Some has partially collapsed under replication scrutiny (ego depletion, positivity ratio). And some was never peer-reviewed science to begin with (Reichheld’s original NPS claim, much of the balanced scorecard literature). Harnish himself has acknowledged that some of his observations are “not scientific at all.”

This brief examines the actual evidence base across eight domains so you can decide where the science is solid, where it is suggestive, and where it is missing.

1 Meeting Cadence Science

1.1 Daily Standups and Coordination

Key researchers: Stray, Sjøberg & Dybå (2016); European Journal of Work and Organizational Psychology (2025)

Core finding: Daily standup meetings are directly and positively related to psychological safety, and indirectly related to work satisfaction and team performance via psychological safety as a mediator. The mechanism: accountability, predictability, task-related feedback, and common knowledge creation.
Limitation: The #1 factor contributing to negative attitudes toward daily standups was the frequency being perceived as too high. Most research comes from Agile software teams, not retail. The relationship is not linear—there is likely an optimal frequency depending on task interdependence and team size.

1.2 Communication Rhythm and Alignment

Researchers: Brown et al. (2021), multi-team systems research

Explicit coordination cannot occur without communication. A common time frame for organizing interactions is essential for team effectiveness. The rhythmic time structure of interactions appears to be a natural feature of effective team coordination, suggesting imposed meeting rhythms may simply formalize what high-performing teams do naturally.

1.3 Cognitive Load and the “Three Questions”

Researchers: George A. Miller (1956); Nelson Cowan (2001); John Sweller (1988)

Miller established working memory holds ~7 ± 2 items. Cowan (2001) revised this to approximately 4 items when controlling for chunking. Sweller’s Cognitive Load Theory showed working memory can only process limited information elements simultaneously.

Connection to Scaling Up: Harnish’s daily huddle prescribes exactly three agenda items (“What’s up,” “Daily metrics,” “Where are you stuck”), aligning with the ~4 item cognitive limit. He does not cite cognitive load theory directly—this is likely intuitive design that happens to align with the science.

2 Goal-Setting Science (The “Rocks” Foundation)

2.1 Locke & Latham’s Goal-Setting Theory

Researchers: Edwin Locke & Gary Latham (1990 book; 2002 retrospective in American Psychologist)

96%
of studies confirm: specific, difficult goals outperform vague “do your best” goals
Locke & Latham (2002) — validated across 100+ tasks, 40,000+ participants, 8+ countries
ComparisonEffect Size (d)Meaning
Hard goals vs. easy goals0.52–0.82Medium to large effect
Specific difficult vs. “do your best”0.42–0.80“Do your best” is nearly as bad as no goal

Key moderators: Goal commitment (public commitment helps), self-efficacy, feedback (goals + feedback outperform either alone), task complexity (effect strongest for simple tasks), and participation (assigned goals work if rationale is given).

This is one of the most validated theories in all of organizational psychology. The Rocks concept sits on the strongest research foundation of any element in Scaling Up.

2.2 SMART vs. FAST Goals

Researcher: Donald Sull, MIT Sloan (2018)

Based on analyzing 500,000+ goals, Sull argued SMART goals “undervalue ambition, focus narrowly on individual performance, and ignore the importance of discussing goals throughout the year.” FAST goals (Frequently discussed, Ambitious, Specific, Transparent) better align with goal-setting research.

Key insight: The “A” in SMART (Achievable) directly contradicts Locke & Latham’s finding that difficult goals outperform easy ones. SMART goals may be inadvertently encouraging mediocrity.

2.3 The Goal Gradient Effect: Why 13 Weeks Beats 52 Weeks

Researchers: Clark Hull (1932, 1934); Kivetz, Urminsky & Zheng (2006)

Hull’s hypothesis: effort expenditure increases as distance to a goal decreases. Kivetz et al. (2006) validated this in human behavior—people accelerate effort as they approach completion. A 13-week quarterly goal creates the gradient effect sooner and more frequently than a 52-week annual goal. By week 8 of 13, you are psychologically “close” to the finish line. By week 8 of 52, you have 44 weeks left and motivation is flat.

2.4 Optimal Number of Goals

The 3–5 “Rocks” prescription aligns with Cowan’s revised working memory capacity (~4 items). Three is probably better than five for a small team, given cognitive limits on attention allocation across goals.

2.5 Baumeister’s Willpower Research — DISPUTED

Replication crisis: The original ego depletion meta-analysis (2010) showed d = 0.62. A coordinated replication across 23 laboratories and 2,100+ participants failed to replicate the effect. Ego depletion became one of the most prominent casualties of psychology’s replication crisis. Newer research suggests what looks like depletion is actually a shift in motivation, not resource exhaustion.

Any Scaling Up advice about “willpower” or “decision fatigue” as resource depletion should be treated with skepticism. The goal-prioritization advice (fewer goals = better) is supported by cognitive load theory, but not by willpower depletion.

3 KPI / Scorecard Science

3.1 Kaplan & Norton’s Balanced Scorecard

Researchers: Robert Kaplan & David Norton (1992 in HBR, 1996 book)

The four perspectives (Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth) were developed from a one-year project with 12 companies. Widely adopted (~50% of Fortune 1000).

30-year retrospective (Hoque, 2022): Rigorous empirical evidence of BSC’s causal impact on firm performance is limited. Most evidence is case studies, not controlled research. The framework is widely used despite a thin empirical base.

3.2 “What Gets Measured Gets Managed” — A Myth

MYTH
Drucker never said this. The Drucker Institute confirmed it.
1956
V. F. Ridgway — who was WARNING against overreliance on measurement

The actual source, V. F. Ridgway (1956), was cautioning: “What gets measured gets managed—even when it’s pointless to measure and manage it, and even if it harms the purpose of the organisation to do so.” BCG research confirmed that excessive measurement actually decreases business results.

3.3 Feedback Loops in Systems Theory

Peter Senge (1990), The Fifth Discipline: A KPI without a feedback loop is just a number. The value of measurement lies in whether it connects to action. The delay between action and result is critical—if too long, the loop breaks down and people stop believing the metric matters.

3.4 The Hawthorne Effect — More Complicated Than Believed

Levitt & List re-analyzed the original Hawthorne data and found effects were weaker than claimed. Original studies had severe methodological flaws: 5–6 subjects, no proper controls. Making metrics visible does affect behavior, but the mechanism is likely social accountability, not simple observation.

3.5 “One Metric That Matters”

Consistent with cognitive load research and choice overload literature (Iyengar & Lepper, 2000), but this is practitioner wisdom, not peer-reviewed science. The concept of Goodhart’s Law applies: “When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.”

Bottom line: The “what gets measured gets managed” philosophy that pervades Scaling Up is built on a misattributed quote and research that warns against exactly this approach. Metrics work when they close feedback loops to action. Metrics without action create cynicism.

4 Strategy Simplification (One-Page Plan)

4.1 The Strategy Execution Problem

90%
of organizations fail to execute strategic plans (Kaplan & Norton)
50%
gap between how clearly execs think strategy is communicated vs. employee reception
2/3
of executive teams are less aligned than they believe

Strategy execution fails due to complexity and misalignment, not bad strategy.

4.2 Cognitive Load Applied to Strategy

Sweller (1988): Working memory handles limited elements. A 50-page plan overwhelms; a one-page plan stays within limits. This is a reasonable theoretical extrapolation, but there are no controlled studies directly comparing one-page vs. multi-page strategic plans on execution success.

4.3 Commander’s Intent

Military doctrine, popularized by Heath & Heath (2007). A clear statement of why and what success looks like enables autonomous decisions when conditions change. “No plan survives first contact with the enemy” (von Moltke). Well-documented in military and high-reliability organization contexts; business evidence is case-study based.

For a 5-person team: A single sentence of intent may be more powerful than a one-page plan. At 5 people, shared mental models are built through daily interaction—the primary coordination mechanism. The OPSP becomes most valuable beyond 15–20 people where natural coordination breaks down.

5 Team Feedback Science

5.1 Kluger & DeNisi (1996) — The Foundational Meta-Analysis

Sample: 607 effect sizes from 23,663 observations

38%
of feedback interventions actually decreased performance
Kluger & DeNisi (1996), Psychological Bulletin, 119(2), 254–284

Feedback improved performance on average (d = 0.41), but the variance was enormous. Their Feedback Intervention Theory explains why:

Attention LevelFocusEffect on Performance
Task learning“Here’s how to do it better”Positive
Task motivation“You’re behind / ahead”Mixed
Self / meta-task“You are a good/bad performer”Negative
The most dangerous insight in this entire brief: Task-focused feedback helps. Self-focused feedback harms. Most managers naturally deliver self-level feedback (“You did great!” or “You need to improve”). Implementing Scaling Up’s feedback-heavy approach without understanding this distinction is like prescribing medication without understanding dosage.

5.2 Edmondson (1999) — Psychological Safety

Sample: 51 work teams in a manufacturing company

Psychological safety (shared belief the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking) predicts learning behavior, which mediates the safety–performance relationship. Team efficacy alone was not significant when controlling for psychological safety. Won the Academy of Management Outstanding Publication Award (2000).

Implication: You cannot build an effective feedback system in a psychologically unsafe environment. Safety must come before feedback, not after.

5.3 Google’s Project Aristotle

Sample: 180+ Google teams

Psychological safety was the #1 predictor, explaining 43% of variance in team performance. Five dynamics ranked: (1) Psychological Safety, (2) Dependability, (3) Structure & Clarity, (4) Meaning, (5) Impact.

Limitation: Not peer-reviewed. Google teams are highly atypical (knowledge workers, high autonomy, generous resources). Generalizability to retail is uncertain.

5.4 Losada & Heaphy (2004) — Positivity Ratio — RETRACTED

RETRACTED: Original claim of a precise 2.9013:1 positivity-to-negativity ratio for high-performing teams. Brown, Sokal & Friedman (2013) demonstrated the mathematical modeling was “entirely unfounded.” The model was formally withdrawn. Losada did not defend it. The broad observation that high-performing teams have more positive interactions survives as a pattern; the precise ratio was pseudomathematics.

5.5 Hackman’s Conditions for Team Effectiveness

Five conditions: Real Team, Compelling Direction, Enabling Structure, Supportive Context, Expert Coaching.

Core insight: Leaders have greatest leverage before work begins—designing the team and its context—not through continuous monitoring. The ongoing feedback that Scaling Up emphasizes is Hackman’s fifth condition (coaching), but the first four conditions must be in place first.

5.6 Feedback Frequency Research

Park, Johnson, Moon & Lee (2019): Feedback frequency interacts with specificity—high frequency + high specificity = best outcomes. High frequency + low specificity = negative outcomes.

Lam et al. (2011): The relationship between feedback frequency and performance is likely curvilinear—optimal at moderate frequency. Too little is ineffective; too much is overwhelming.

5.7 Peer vs. Manager Feedback

Peer feedback boosts performance by up to 14% (Gartner). Employees are 3.6x more motivated with daily vs. annual manager feedback (Gallup). Social feedback systems provide 50+ touchpoints/year vs. 1–2 traditional reviews.

Synthesis for a 5-person team: (a) Psychological safety first, before any feedback system. (b) Always task-focused (“The display in aisle 3 increased sales by X”), never self-focused (“You’re doing great”). (c) Peer feedback is especially valuable since people work side by side. (d) Moderate frequency—daily huddles for coordination, weekly for reflection.

6 Customer Feedback Science

6.1 NPS — Weak Original Science

Reichheld (2003) claimed NPS was “the one number you need to grow.”

Failed independent replication: Keiningham, Aksoy & Cooil replicated with a larger sample and found NPS “performs no better than other measures” (CSAT, CES) at predicting growth. The original study used cross-sectional data; the “one number” claim was never empirically validated in a peer-reviewed journal. NPS’s popularity is driven by simplicity, not scientific superiority.

6.2 Closed-Loop Feedback (Systems Theory)

From Senge and Deming: the type of metric matters less than whether the loop is closed. A mediocre metric with a closed loop outperforms a perfect metric with an open loop. This is the single most important insight for customer feedback: any measurement system that triggers visible action will work. NPS, CSAT, CES, even a simple thumbs up/down—the loop matters more than the scale.

6.3 The Kano Model

Researcher: Noriaki Kano (1984)

Core insight: Customer satisfaction is not linear. Three categories:

Must-Be features: Cause dissatisfaction when absent, only neutral when present (clean store, correct prices).
Performance features: Proportional—more is better (product variety, staff friendliness).
Delighters: Create disproportionate satisfaction but no penalty for absence (unexpected gift wrapping, personalized recommendations).

Asking “what do you want?” systematically misses Delighters because customers don’t know to ask for what they haven’t experienced.

6.4 Jobs-to-be-Done (Christensen)

People “hire” products to get a job done. 75–85% of new products fail because they target demographics, not jobs. The “milkshake study” is the canonical example. Primarily case-study evidence, not experimental, but influential in product development.

6.5 The Service-Profit Chain

Researchers: Heskett, Sasser & Schlesinger (1994, 1997, Harvard Business School)

The chain: Employee satisfaction → Service quality → Customer satisfaction → Profit.

Critique: Silvestro & Cross (2000) found that simultaneous empirical validation of all linkages in the chain was lacking. Causal direction is questionable—does employee satisfaction cause customer satisfaction, or does a profitable business make everyone happier?
Bottom line for customer feedback: Skip the metric debate. Use whatever is simplest (even a 5-star rating). Close every loop: every piece of feedback should trigger visible action within 48 hours. The Kano Model is the most useful mental framework—think in terms of Must-Be, Performance, and Delighter categories.

7 Employee Engagement Science

True-score correlation with composite performance: r = 0.43. Top-half units nearly double their odds of success vs. bottom-half. The 28% reduction in shrinkage is particularly relevant for retail.

Limitations: Gallup researches its own proprietary instrument (conflict of interest). Causality direction uncertain—do engaged employees create better outcomes, or do better workplaces create engagement? Q12 is proprietary, limiting independent replication.

7.2 Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

Three basic psychological needs:

NeedDefinitionRetail Application
AutonomySense of volition and choiceLet employees decide how to arrange their section, choose cross-sell pairings
CompetenceFeeling effective, experiencing masterySkill-building, product knowledge, seeing KPI improvement
RelatednessConnection, belongingDaily huddle, team celebrations, shared purpose

Meta-analyses confirm consistent associations with job satisfaction, engagement, and proactive behavior. Intrinsic motivation is most associated with active engagement and negatively with counterproductive behavior.

7.3 Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory

Core insight: Satisfaction and dissatisfaction are separate continuums, not opposite ends of one scale.

Hygiene factors (salary, conditions, policies, schedule): Prevent dissatisfaction but don’t create motivation.
Motivators (achievement, recognition, the work itself, responsibility): Create satisfaction and drive performance.

Engagement programs focused on perks (free snacks, team outings) address the wrong continuum. They prevent dissatisfaction at best.
Limitation: Narrow original sample (200 accountants and engineers in Pittsburgh). Replication with other methods is inconsistent. But the core insight—fixing bad conditions doesn’t create motivation—is broadly supported.

7.4 Flow Theory (Csik­szent­mihályi)

Optimal experience occurs when: clear goals exist, immediate feedback is available, and skills are matched to challenge level. Workplace paradox: people experience more flow at work than leisure, but wish they were elsewhere—suggesting flow without a sense of choice coexists with resentment.

Connection to Scaling Up: Autonomy is the weakest link in the framework—Scaling Up is more prescriptive than autonomy-supportive. The focus on purpose and achievement (motivators) is correct per Herzberg. The daily huddle may support relatedness and competence (SDT) while potentially undermining autonomy if perceived as surveillance.

8 The “Rhythm” Concept

8.1 Ultradian Rhythms in Work Performance

Researchers: Nathaniel Kleitman (1950s–60s); Peretz Lavie, Technion; Anders Ericsson

The 90-minute sleep cycle continues throughout the day as the Basic Rest-Activity Cycle (BRAC). After 75–90 minutes of focused work, the body enters a 15–20 minute rest phase. Lavie’s key finding: the ultradian trough is not optional. Ericsson independently found elite practitioners across domains work in 60–90 minute focused bursts, not marathon sessions.

8.2 Planning Frequency and Cognitive Function

This mirrors Kluger & DeNisi’s three feedback levels: daily aligns with task-learning feedback, weekly with task-motivation, quarterly with meta-task (strategy).

8.3 Battle Rhythm in High-Reliability Organizations

“A deliberate daily cycle of command, staff, and unit activities intended to synchronize current and future operations.”

Four properties: Cascading (tempo set from top), Aligning (units coordinate by knowing each other’s rhythm), Disciplined (maintained especially when disrupted), Learning-integrated (after-action reviews built in).

Key insight: The rhythm itself is the coordination mechanism. If every team operates on the same cycle, the organization self-synchronizes without centralized control. Consistency of timing matters as much as content—same time every day, without exception.
For a 5-person team: The rhythm concept may be the single most valuable Scaling Up element at this size. Daily 5-minute huddle at shift start, weekly 30-minute review, quarterly half-day for 3 priorities. The consistency creates the coordination—skipping a single daily huddle “because we’re busy” is precisely when it matters most.

Where to Dig Deeper

Based on the evidence quality analysis, the most productive areas for further research are:

  1. Feedback Intervention Theory (Kluger & DeNisi) — The strongest and most actionable science in the entire framework. Understanding the task-vs-self distinction could transform how you run every meeting and conversation.
  2. Goal-Setting Theory (Locke & Latham) — The most validated element. Worth understanding the moderators deeply, especially for complex/creative tasks where the effect weakens.
  3. Psychological Safety (Edmondson) — The prerequisite for everything else. Without this, Rocks and feedback systems will fail.
  4. Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan) — The engagement engine. Understanding autonomy/competence/relatedness could help design the daily rhythm in a way that motivates rather than surveils.
  5. The Kano Model — The most underused framework for customer understanding. More useful than NPS for actually improving the customer experience.
