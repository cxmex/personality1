I The Rockefeller Habits Meeting Rhythm

Verne Harnish’s Scaling Up (2014) distills 20+ years of coaching growth companies into a structured meeting cadence. His core principle: “To move faster, pulse faster.” The rhythm creates a heartbeat for the organization—daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annually.

1.1 Daily Huddle (Junta Diaria de Pie)

The daily huddle is a standing meeting (never seated) lasting under 15 minutes, held at the same time every day. For Plaza Teresa, recommended at 9:45 AM (15 minutes before opening).

Daily Huddle — 3 Blocks, 5 Minutes Each

5 min “¿Qué hay hoy?” — Each person in 30 seconds names their key activities for the next 24 hours: deliveries, decisions, critical tasks.
5 min “Números del día” — Yesterday’s sales total, customer traffic, returns, any anomalies.
5 min “¿Dónde estás atorado?” — Each person shares one obstacle blocking their progress today.
Critical rule: The huddle is NOT for solving problems. If a topic needs discussion, it is noted and resolved between the relevant people afterward. Harnish estimates a well-run daily huddle saves 1 hour per person per day in ad-hoc interruptions, emails, and miscommunication.

1.2 Weekly Meeting (60–90 Minutes)

The weekly meeting is the execution engine. Recommended: Monday at 9:00 AM.

Weekly Meeting Agenda

5 min Good news — Each person shares one personal or professional win. Connects the team as humans.
10 min Scorecard / KPIs — Review weekly numbers (see Section 1.4). “Was it a good week or a bad week?”—answered with data, not opinions.
10 min Rocks review — Each quarterly priority marked green/yellow/red. Discuss yellows and reds.
10 min Customer & employee feedback — NPS scores, specific stories, recurring complaints, employee observations from the sales floor.
10 min Start/Stop/Keep round — Each person contributes 1 item per category (see Part II).
30–60 min IDS (Identify → Discuss → Solve) — Pick 1–2 critical issues and resolve them fully. One issue solved well beats ten discussed superficially.
5 min Action items + cascade messages — Who does what by when. What to communicate to the rest of the team.

1.5 Rocks and the 13-Week Race

A “Rock” is a quarterly priority that, if completed, moves the business significantly. The name comes from Covey’s analogy: fill the jar with big rocks first, then sand fills the gaps. Maximum 3–5 Rocks per quarter for the company, 1–3 per person.

Example Rocks for Plaza Teresa (Q3 2026)

1Implement customer loyalty programHoracio200 customers enrolled by quarter end
2Reduce inventory shrinkage from 4% to 2%LuisShrinkage < 2% at quarter close

Each Rock is decomposed into 13 weekly milestones. The 13-Week Race creates urgency: a quarter is long enough to achieve something meaningful but short enough to maintain pressure. Rocks are binary at quarter end—complete or not.

II Start/Stop/Keep: Structured Team Feedback

2.1 Origins and Theory

The SKS form (Stop-Keep-Start) was created by Phil Daniels, a psychology professor at Brigham Young University, and later popularized by Thomas J. DeLong at Harvard Business School in his 2011 Harvard Business Review article “Three Questions for Effective Feedback.”

The framework draws on Feedback Intervention Theory (Kluger & DeNisi, 1996), which meta-analyzed 607 effect sizes across 23,663 observations: feedback improves performance on average (d = 0.41), but 38% of feedback interventions actually decreased performance. The key moderator: feedback works when it is task-focused rather than self-focused. SSK succeeds because it directs attention to specific behaviors, not personal identity.

For Plaza Teresa: rotate 1-on-1 SSK conversations so every employee is heard individually every 5 weeks. Bring key insights to the team meeting.

III Employee Pulse Surveys: Frequency Science

3.1 Pulse vs. Annual Surveys

Traditional annual engagement surveys take 20–30 minutes and produce stale data. By the time results are analyzed, disengagement has been costing money for months. Pulse surveys are shorter (3–10 questions), more frequent, and designed for real-time sentiment tracking.

DimensionAnnual SurveyPulse Survey
Length30–60 questions, 20–30 min3–10 questions, 2–3 min
Frequency1–2x/yearWeekly to monthly
ActionabilityLow (stale data)High (real-time)
BenchmarkingStrongWeak
Fatigue riskModerate (length)Moderate (frequency)

3.2 Optimal Frequency

The critical insight: Frequency matters less than the organization’s ability to act on results quickly. Weekly only works if you can review and respond within 7 days. Surveying without acting actively decreases engagement—it is worse than not surveying at all.
CadenceQuestionsBest ForAct Within
Weekly1–3Rapid-change, high-trust environments7 days
Biweekly3–5Most teams (balanced signal-to-noise)14 days
Monthly5–10Deeper exploration, lower-trust30 days

Recommendation for Plaza Teresa: Biweekly cadence with 3–5 questions. Small teams experience faster sentiment shifts; a 5-person team leader can realistically act within 2 weeks; monthly is too slow to catch brewing interpersonal issues.

3.3 The Survey Fatigue Paradox

65% → 25%
Response rate collapse when organizations survey without acting
Culture Amp: “People don’t get survey fatigue, they get lack-of-action fatigue.”

McKinsey’s review of 20+ academic articles confirmed: perceived lack of action is the primary driver of participation decline. Recovery is possible: when organizations implement visible action-response loops, response rates can rebound from 25% to 58% in a single quarter.

The Action-Response Loop

  1. Acknowledge — Share aggregate results within 48 hours
  2. Prioritize — Identify 1–2 actionable themes (not everything)
  3. Commit — State one specific action publicly
  4. Act — Implement before the next survey
  5. Report back — “Last time you said X, we did Y”

3.4 Psychological Safety Measurement

Amy Edmondson’s (1999) 7-item scale, validated across industries and cultures, measures the team-level belief that interpersonal risk-taking is safe. For pulse use, 2 items carry the strongest signal:

ItemEnglishSpanish
Core item“It is safe to take a risk on this team.”“En este equipo es seguro tomar riesgos o probar cosas nuevas.”
Problems item“Members can bring up problems and tough issues.”“En este equipo podemos hablar de problemas difíciles abiertamente.”
Valued item“My unique skills and talents are valued.”“Mis habilidades y talentos son valorados en este equipo.”
Mistakes (R)“If I make a mistake, it is held against me.”“Si cometo un error, se usa en mi contra.”

3.5 eNPS for Small Teams

Employee Net Promoter Score: “On a scale of 0–10, how likely are you to recommend this company as a place to work?” Same calculation as customer NPS.

Small team caution: With 5 employees, eNPS is mathematically volatile—one person shifting from Promoter to Detractor swings the score by 40 points. Use eNPS quarterly only, always paired with an open-ended follow-up, and track the trend across quarters rather than individual readings. Never segment by demographics at n=5.

3.6 Small Team Anonymity

With 5 employees, true anonymity is nearly impossible (writing style, specific complaints can reveal identity). Mexican workplace culture has high power distance (Hofstede)—employees may hesitate to give negative feedback to a boss.

Recommended approach: Numeric ratings collected digitally (shown as team averages only) + open weekly discussion + private /sugerencias channel for sensitive topics. Start anonymous and evolve to open as confianza builds. Manager models vulnerability by sharing their own ratings first.

IV Customer NPS at Point of Sale via QR Code

4.1 NPS Fundamentals

Fred Reichheld introduced NPS in his 2003 Harvard Business Review article “The One Number You Need to Grow,” developed with Bain & Company. One question replaces complex satisfaction surveys with a high-signal metric.

“En una escala del 0 al 10, ¿qué tan probable es que recomiendes Plaza Teresa a un amigo o familiar?”
ScoreCategoryBehavior
9–10PromotersLoyal enthusiasts who keep buying and refer others
7–8PassivesSatisfied but vulnerable to competitors
0–6DetractorsUnhappy; can damage brand through negative word-of-mouth

NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors (range: −100 to +100). Score above 50 = excellent for Mexico retail.

4.4 Response Rate Optimization

  1. Verbal prompt from staff — “¿Nos ayudas con tu opinión? Solo toma 30 segundos.” This alone doubles scan rates.
  2. Show estimated time on signage (“30 segundos”)
  3. No login required — zero friction entry
  4. Mobile-optimized — large tap targets
  5. Small incentive (optional) — 5% next-visit discount

4.5 Detractor Recovery: The Service Recovery Paradox

95%
of customers will return if a complaint is resolved quickly
Hart, Heskett & Sasser (1990), Harvard Business Review

Customers who experience a service failure that is excellently resolved often become more loyal than those who never had a problem. This makes detractor recovery one of the highest-ROI activities in customer service.

Alert Protocol

  1. Instant alert — WhatsApp notification to store manager when score ≤ 6
  2. Acknowledge (within minutes) — “Gracias por tu comentario. Lamento que tu experiencia no fue la mejor.”
  3. Own the issue — No excuses. “Tienes razón, eso no debió pasar.”
  4. Fix it — Replacement, discount, or personal attention on next visit
  5. Follow up — “¿Pudimos resolver tu inquietud?”

V Consumer Feedback Loops: Closed-Loop Systems

5.1 The Closed-Loop Cycle: DAFA

A closed-loop feedback system guarantees that every piece of customer feedback triggers a defined organizational response. Unlike open-loop systems (collect but never act), a closed loop circles back to the customer.

StageWhatWhoWhen
DetectCapture feedback at moment of experienceAll channelsContinuously
AcknowledgeRespond to customer within SLAStaff / Manager< 2 hours (digital), immediately (in-person)
Fix / ActRoute to owner, implement fix, documentIssue owner< 48 hours
Follow-upCircle back to confirm resolutionManager1–3 days after fix

Outer loop (strategic)Aggregate patternsWeeks to monthsMultiple customers mention long wait times; add second register during peak

How to Ask Without Being Pushy

  • Identify peak satisfaction moments — after a great product recommendation, after resolving a problem, after a repeat customer compliment
  • Make it personal: “Me da mucho gusto que le haya gustado. Si tiene un momento, una reseña en Google nos ayuda muchísimo como negocio pequeño.”
  • Make it effortless: Hand them a card with a QR code
  • Never incentivize scores — violates Google policies
  • Target: 1–2 new reviews per week, manager responds to ALL within 24 hours

5.7 WhatsApp for Feedback in Mexico

With 77 million users and 93% penetration, WhatsApp is Mexico’s primary digital communication infrastructure. WhatsApp Business App (free) supports up to 50 quick replies and broadcast lists of 256 contacts.

Quick Replies to Set Up

  • /mejora — “Nos encantaría mejorar. ¿Qué podríamos hacer diferente?”
  • /google — “Tu opinión nos ayuda mucho! Puedes dejarnos una reseña aquí: [link]”

Post-Purchase Flow (High-Value Items)

  • Day 0: “Gracias por tu compra! Si necesitas algo, estamos aquí.”
  • Day 3: “¿Cómo te ha ido con [product]? Queremos asegurarnos de que todo esté bien.”
  • Day 7: If positive, send Google Review request.

6.3 Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1–2) — Cost: $15–30 USD

  • Start daily huddle at 9:45 AM — week 1 only the “stuck” question (5 min), week 2 add all 3 questions
  • Start weekly meeting (Monday 9 AM, 30 min initially)
  • Create NPS Google Form (3 questions) + generate QR code
  • Print QR code stands for counter and exit
  • Set up Google Business Profile short link
  • Set up WhatsApp Business with greeting + quick replies
  • Create feedback tracking Google Sheet
  • Introduce SSK concept to team with paper cards

Phase 2: Build Rhythm (Weeks 3–8)

  • Full daily huddle (15 min) + full weekly meeting (60 min)
  • Launch NPS QR at checkout — staff verbally prompts every customer
  • Set up detractor alerts (WhatsApp notification for score ≤ 6)
  • First biweekly employee pulse survey (3 questions)
  • Train team on LEARN service recovery model
  • Empower $200 MXN resolution authority per employee
  • Close the loop: report SSK actions back to team weekly
  • Begin Google Review requests from promoters

Phase 3: Optimize (Month 3–6)

  • First quarterly planning: define 3 Rocks, set Critical Number, build 13-week race board
  • Full quarterly SSK with anonymous digital submission
  • Add Edmondson psychological safety items to quarterly pulse
  • Track NPS trend weekly — set monthly targets
  • Implement one systemic improvement per week from outer loop
  • Rotate SSK format every 4 weeks (Plus/Delta, 4Ls)
  • Post-purchase WhatsApp follow-up for high-value items
  • Quarterly eNPS measurement

Phase 4: Scale (Month 6+)

  • Build NPS endpoint into FastAPI app with Supabase storage
  • Automated detractor alerts via Twilio/WhatsApp Business API
  • NPS dashboard integrated with weekly meeting display
  • Transition to named employee feedback as trust solidifies
  • Begin peer-to-peer SSK (not just team-to-manager)
  • Quarterly relational NPS via WhatsApp broadcast to repeat customers
  • SEO impact tracking from Google Reviews
